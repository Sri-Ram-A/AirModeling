Derivation — step by step
Step 1 — Distance & Bearing
Haversine great-circle distance and initial bearing from source to receptor.
$d = 2R\arcsin\!\sqrt{\sin^2\!\tfrac{\Delta\varphi}{2}+\cos\varphi_1\cos\varphi_2\sin^2\!\tfrac{\Delta\lambda}{2}}$
Step 2 — Wind-frame projection
Rotate source→receptor vector into downwind / crosswind axes using the angle between wind direction and bearing.
$x = d\cos(\theta_w - \theta_b),\quad y = d\sin(\theta_w - \theta_b)$
Step 3 — Dispersion widths (Pasquill-Gifford)
Power-law parameterisation; coefficients depend on stability class (Turner, EPA 1970).
$\sigma_y = a_y\,x^{b_y},\qquad \sigma_z = \min\!\left(a_z\,x^{b_z},\;500\right)$
Step 4 — Ground-level Gaussian plume
Ground reflection (image source) simplifies the vertical term to 2exp(−H²/2σz²) / (2π…) → the factor of π.
$T_{ij} = \frac{1}{\pi\,u\,\sigma_y\sigma_z}\exp\!\left(-\frac{y^2}{2\sigma_y^2}\right)\exp\!\left(-\frac{H^2}{2\sigma_z^2}\right)\times 10^6$

Interpretation guide

High wind speed → larger denominator → lower T. Plume is diluted faster along the downwind axis.

Unstable (A/B) → large σ_y, σ_z → wide plume but lower peak concentration on centreline.

Stable (E/F) → narrow plume, high centreline T but falls off sharply sideways.

Stack height H → vertical Gaussian exp(−H²/2σ_z²) attenuates ground-level concentration. Higher stack = lower T at ground.

Upwind receptor → x ≤ 0 → T = 0 regardless of distance. Physical necessity: steady-state plume cannot travel upstream.
